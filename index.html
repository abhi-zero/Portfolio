Journey

College Dayz Tutorial Hell Now

The Post-COVID Coders Club

I started coding in college because I chose to pursue a Bachelor of Computer Applications (BCA). My admission took place post-COVID, which meant adjusting to a new normal of learning. But let me tell you, the best year was my 3rd year!

There was this girl I talked to daily—don’t worry, not a love story, just a fun one. And I teamed up with friends to build my first project: a VB.NET Grocery Store Management System.

  1. Staff ranking and info
  2. Dynamic stock updates
  3. Billing system (print bills only)
  4. Auto-decrease in stock quantity after purchases

That project didn’t just teach us coding—it taught us teamwork, laughter, and the joy of finally getting something to work after hours of debugging.

Tutorial Hell—Population: Me

After graduation, I decided I wanted to become a frontend developer. So, I went back to the basics—HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. For the first 10-15 days, I felt like I was learning... but then I realized I was just watching tutorials without practicing.

It was a cycle:

  1. Watch tutorial.
  2. Take notes.
  3. Feel productive.
  4. Repeat—without ever writing actual code!

Then one day, I stumbled upon a YouTuber who said, 'Make projects, even if they’re garbage.' And that hit me! I realized that building bad projects is still better than watching hours of tutorials. So, I rolled up my sleeves and started making things, even if they were imperfect.

Imperfect Projects, Perfect Progress

These days, I follow a simple mantra:

  1. Do a little research.
  2. Start building, no matter how messy.
  3. Get stuck (because I always do).
  4. Watch tutorials or search for solutions.
  5. Refactor later.

Sure, my projects might not be perfect at first, but they get better as I go. Every mistake is a lesson, every refactor is progress. Little by little, I’m getting there—and having fun along the way.
